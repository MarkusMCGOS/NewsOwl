EXPLAINER: How the U.S. Government Works

By Adam Shen
Contributions and Edits by Markus Lau
12/05/2024

The U.S. government has a very distinct structure. It has 3 branches created by the constitution, which each control a separate part of the government. The constitution also states that all 3 branches must have equal power for balance. The branches are the legislative branch, executive branch, and judicial branch.

Legislative

The legislative branch uses a bicameral system, meaning that it comprises two chambers, the House and the Senate. The legislative branch’s main purpose is to create and pass laws, approve budgets and foreign policies, monitor other branches, and manage the armed forces.

The House of Representatives

There are a total of 435 members, with the number of representatives based on the population of their state—for example, California has 52, meanwhile Wyoming, with a much smaller population, only has 1 person as their representative. Each representative serves 2 year terms, with the ability to re-run indefinitely every other year. These are the requirements to be a representative:

  • Must be at least 25 years old
  • Must have been a U.S. citizen for at least 7 years
  • Must be a resident of the state they represent

Responsibilities exclusive to the House:

  • Initiate revenue bills, tax bills and budget bills
  • Initate impeachment of federal officials (including the President)
  • Elect the President in case of an electoral college tie

The Senate

There are a total of 100 members in the Senate, with 2 senators per state, no matter the population. Each senator serves 6 year terms, also with the ability to re-run indefinitely. However, every other year, a third of the Senate is up for grabs. The Vice President is the head of the Senate, but may only cast a vote for a tie-breaker. To run for Senate, you must:

  • Must be at least 30 years old
  • Must have been a U.S. citizen for at least 9 years
  • Must be a resident of the state they represent

Responsibilities exclusive to the Senate:

  • Approve treaties
  • Approve presidential appointments
  • Try impeached officials

Both chambers of the Legislative Branch have a large ability and responsibility. They can tax, declare war, regulate foreign affairs, and establish laws.

Executive

The president heads the Executive Branch, and has unique abilities. For example, they can give executive orders, make vetoes on bills passed by Congress and appoint federal judges and heads of federal agencies (president's cabinet). However, the Senate must vote to actually confirm those members of the president's cabinet, to ensure transparency and balance.

The president serves a 4 year term, with the ability to re-run for a second term. The president must be at least 35 years old, a natural born citizen, and have lived in the U.S. for at least 14 years.

The president is also the Commander in Chief of the armed forces, and can make treaties with other countries. However, the Senate must approve those treaties.

If the president is unable to continue to be president for any reason—death, sickness, or for another reason, there is a line of command to be the next President. First is the Vice President, then the Speaker of the House, then the president of the Senate, then the cabinet members down the line.

The president can also be impeached by the House of Representatives, and then tried by the Senate. If the Senate finds the president guilty, they are removed from office.

The Executive Branch includes:

  • The President
    • Head of the Executive Branch
    • Commander in Chief of the Armed Forces
    • Head of State
  • The Vice President
    • Casts vote in Senate in the event of a tie
    • Supports the President
  • The Cabinet
    • Appointed by the President
    • Confirmed by the Senate
    • Includes:
      • Secretary of State
      • Secretary of Defense
      • Secretary of the Treasury
      • Attorney General
      • Secretary of the Interior
      • Secretary of Agriculture
      • Secretary of Commerce
      • Secretary of Labor
      • Secretary of Health and Human Services
      • Secretary of Housing and Urban Development
      • Secretary of Transportation
      • Secretary of Energy
      • Secretary of Education
      • Secretary of Veterans Affairs
      • Secretary of Homeland Security
    • Federal Agencies
      • FBI (Federal Bureau of Investigations)
      • CIA (Central Intelligence Agency)
      • NSA (National Security Agency)
      • EPA (Environmental Protection Agency)
      • FDA (Food and Drug Administration)
      • CDC (Center for Disease Control)
      • NASA (National Aeronautics and Space Administration)

Judicial

The Judicial branch's function can be described in one word: court. Its structure primarily comprises federal courts and judges.

Supreme Court

The Supreme Court is the court with the highest legal authority in the country.The Supreme Court (aka Supreme Court of the United States (SCOTUS)) is composed of 1 Chief Justice and 8 Associate Justices. The Chief Justice’s role is to lead court cases, maintain order in the court, and oversee cases. The Associate Justice’s role is to discuss, deliberate, and cast votes. If a majority of 5 is reached, then the case is resolved. The cases that the Supreme Court discusses are mostly ones with significant legal or constitutional issues that cannot be resolved by a lower court.

Court of Appeals

Next in power are the Court of Appeals, with 13 spread throughout the country. It is important to remember that the courts of appeals do not actually hear new cases. Instead, they review lower court trials to ensure that the law was applied correctly.

District Courts

Then, the district courts. There are 94 district courts, with at least one in each state and territory (such as Puerto Rico, Guam, etc.). These courts can also be referred to as "regular courts" because they are where federal cases are primarily heard.

Special Courts

Finally, there are Special Courts. These courts handle specific types of cases rather than general cases. For example, a tax court only deals with tax-related cases and not anything else. One of the main reasons special courts exist is because of efficiency; when a court specializes in a certain type of case, it becomes more efficient at handling complex issues.


The US government is governed under the constitution, allowing balance in power while maintaining its function and efficiency. The branches include: the legislative branch (law), the executive branch (command), and the judicial branch (court).
